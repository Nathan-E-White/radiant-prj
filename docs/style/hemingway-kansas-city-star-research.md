## Purpose

This note records primary-source research on the Kansas City Star copy/style rules associated with Ernest Hemingway. It is intended to support a public-facing documents and resume style guide for this repo. It is not a general Hemingway writing-advice collection, and it intentionally excludes unsourced blog summaries and secondary listicles.

## Findings

The source to anchor the style guide is the Kansas City Star facsimile titled "The Star Copy Style," not a generic Hemingway-advice list. The facsimile presents page one as a provenance note from The Kansas City Star and page two as the copy-style rule sheet. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF, archived capture above.

The Star's provenance note says the facsimile is one of the most requested pieces of Star-history memorabilia and identifies it as the document Hemingway would have been given while writing police and emergency-room items at The Star in 1917 and 1918. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF, archived capture above.

The Star does not claim perfect certainty about the exact sheet Hemingway held. It says early Star style sheets exist in two different versions, neither can be dated definitively, the version reproduced is believed to be the one used at the newspaper around 1915, and Hemingway likely used either this sheet or a very similar version. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF, archived capture above.

The Star describes the reproduction as the best available copy of the original deteriorated document. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF, archived capture above.

The famous opening rules are: "Use short sentences. Use short first paragraphs. Use vigorous English. Be positive, not negative." Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF, archived capture above.

The sheet is not only a four-rule aphorism. It is a dense copy desk document covering concision, word choice, grammar, sequence of tenses, punctuation style, numbers, attribution, quotation handling, local geography abbreviations, legal/police wording, and internal Star typography. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF, archived capture above.

A repeated principle is deletion of padding. The sheet tells writers to eliminate superfluous words and gives examples that remove filler around times, dates, dollar amounts, purpose phrases, and conversational tags. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF, archived capture above.

The sheet prefers direct, concrete, active wording over ornate phrasing: it discourages extravagant adjectives, stale slang, trite phrases, foreign-language affectation where plain English works, and inflated diction such as formal residence/lives substitutions when simpler words fit. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF, archived capture above.

The sheet is also a precision document. It distinguishes nearby words and constructions, including expect versus anticipate, fewer versus less, if versus whether, each other versus one another, latter versus last, persons versus people in some contexts, and give/contribute versus donate/donation under The Star's house style. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF, archived capture above.

The sheet emphasizes attribution and readable quotation structure. It warns that a long quotation without early speaker identification makes a poor lead and tells writers to break into quotations early enough for the reader to know who is speaking. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF, archived capture above.

The sheet should be treated as a historical source, not imported wholesale. It contains dated, culturally biased, and now-unacceptable terminology and assumptions from its period. For modern Radiant documents, its durable value is concision, clarity, attribution, and precision, not its social vocabulary or every prescriptive usage rule. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF, archived capture above.

## Implications For Radiant Public-Facing Documents

Use the Star sheet as a source-backed argument for a clear, evidence-first style:

- Start with the point. Public pages, resumes, and summaries should open with the result, role, or evidence basis before context. Basis: short first paragraphs and early speaker/source identification in The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF.
- Prefer short sentences with one claim each. Long chained sentences should be split unless the relationship between clauses is essential. Basis: short sentences and superfluous-word deletion in The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF.
- Use active, positive phrasing. Say what the system does, what was built, what changed, or what evidence supports the claim. Avoid defining a capability mainly by what it is not unless the boundary is the point. Basis: vigorous English and positive phrasing in The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF.
- Cut resume padding. Prefer "Built," "Reduced," "Verified," "Shipped," and "Led" over phrases like "was responsible for," "for the purpose of," or "in the course of." Basis: The Star's examples deleting filler around purpose, time, and conversation phrases in the facsimile PDF.
- Avoid hype words and stale jargon. Words like strategic, innovative, robust, cutting-edge, world-class, seamless, and mission-critical need evidence or should disappear. Basis: The Star's warnings against stale slang, trite phrases, and extravagant adjectives in the facsimile PDF.
- Keep claim ownership visible. If a document says a fact comes from a public source, name the source beside the claim rather than burying it in a vague bibliography. Basis: The Star's quotation-attribution guidance in the facsimile PDF.
- Preserve technical distinctions. Do not flatten measured, imputed, simulated, estimated, mocked, and verified into one generic "data" word. Basis: The Star's repeated distinctions between nearby terms and usages in the facsimile PDF.
- Treat the sheet as a style ancestor, not a law book. Use it to justify brevity, plain speech, and source discipline; do not adopt dated terminology or obsolete social categories. Basis: historical content and provenance limitations in The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF.

For a resume-specific guide, the practical translation is: lead with the most concrete result, keep each bullet to one action plus one outcome, name the evidence or scale when it is real, avoid adjective-driven self-praise, and cut every phrase that only says the writer was present.

## Open Questions

- The Star facsimile says Hemingway later made the famous "best rules" remark to a reporter, but the facsimile does not identify the reporter, publication, interview date, or transcript. A stronger public guide should either cite only the Star's facsimile note or locate the original interview/letter before using the quotation as Hemingway's direct testimony. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF.
- The Star says two early versions of the style sheet exist and neither can be dated definitively. A deeper archival pass should compare both versions if a library, Star archive, or Hemingway archive holding exposes them. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF.
- The facsimile says the reproduced sheet is believed to be the one used around 1915 and that Hemingway likely used this or a similar sheet. The guide should preserve that uncertainty and avoid saying this exact sheet is proven to be Hemingway's personal copy. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF.
- The live first-party PDF URL was unstable during this pass. Before publishing an external-facing guide, recheck whether The Kansas City Star has a current canonical page or archive endpoint for the PDF. Source: Internet Archive CDX capture record; original Kansas City Star PDF URL above.
- If the public guide wants to reproduce the full rule sheet or page image, confirm copyright and permission. For now, quote only short excerpts and otherwise paraphrase. Source: The Kansas City Star facsimile PDF.
